Digestive System

What is the digestive system?

The gastrointestinal tract, along with your liver, pancreas, and gallbladder, make up your digestive system. The GI tract is made up of a number of interconnected hollow organs that extend from your mouth to your anus. Your mouth, esophagus, stomach, small intestine, large intestine, and anus are the organs that make up your GI tract, in the order that they are joined.

Whats the function of the Digestive system?

Your digestive system is specially designed to carry out its function of converting food into the nutrients and energy you require to survive. After that, it conveniently packages your solid waste, or stool, for disposal the next time you have a bowel movement.

Why is digestion important?

Because your body needs the nutrients from the food you eat and the liquids you drink to stay healthy and function properly, digestion is crucial. Carbohydrates, proteins, fats, vitamins, minerals, and water are all examples of nutrients. The nutrients in the food and liquids you consume are broken down and absorbed by your digestive system for use in vital processes like cell growth, repair, and energy production.

What organs make up the digestive system?

Organs of the digestive system
Mouth the start of the digestive system. In actuality, digestion begins before taking a bite. As soon as the person smell or see food, the salivary glands start to work. When start eating, chewing food to break it up into pieces to become easier to digest. saliva begins to break down the food into a form that body can absorb and use. The tongue moves the food from mouth into throat and esophagus when swallowing.
Esophagus food travels from mouth to esophagus, which is situated in throat close to the trachea. A tiny flap called epiglottis covers your windpipe while swallowing to prevent choking. Food enters stomach through peristalsis process, which involves a series of muscular contractions in the esophagus, but before food can enter your stomach, the lower esophageal sphincter, which resembles a ring, must relax. The sphincter then tightens, stopping the stomach contents from returning to the esophagus. If it doesn't, acid reflux or heartburn might happen as these contents flow back into the esophagus.
Stomach The stomach is a hollow organ that serves as a "container" for food as it is combined with stomach enzymes. These enzymes continue turning food into a form that can be consumed. The breakdown process is carried out by strong acids and potent enzymes secreted by cells in the lining of the stomach. The stomach's contents are released into the small intestine once they have been sufficiently processed.
Small intestine The small intestine, which consists of the duodenum, jejunum, and ileum, is a muscular tube measuring 22 feet in length and used to break down food using pancreatic enzymes and bile from the liver. The movement of food through this organ while it is being mixed with pancreatic and liver juices during peristalsis is another function of this organ.
Pancreas Digestive enzymes are secreted into the duodenum by the pancreas, where they break down protein, fats, and carbohydrates. Additionally, the pancreas produces insulin and delivers it directly into the blood. The primary hormone in the body for metabolizing sugar is insulin.
Liver Although the liver performs a variety of tasks in the digestive system, processing the nutrients taken up from the small intestine is its primary duty. Digesting fat and some vitamins requires the help of bile, which the liver secretes into the small intestine.
Gallbladder Bile from the liver is concentrated and stored in the gallbladder before being released into the duodenum of the small intestine to aid in the absorption and digestion of fats.
Colon In order to make it simple and convenient to empty your bowels, the colon is in charge of processing waste. The muscular tube that joins the small intestine to the rectum is 6 feet long.
Rectum The 8-inch-long, straight chamber known as the rectum joins the colon to the anus. The rectum's duties include collecting stool from the colon, alerting the person to the presence of stool, and holding the stool until evacuation occurs. Sensors alert the brain when anything (such as gas or stool) enters the rectum. The decision to release the rectal contents is then made by the brain.
Anus The last section of the digestive system is the anus. The pelvic floor muscles and the two anal sphincters are located in a 2-inch canal (internal and external). Rectal contents can be felt by the upper anus lining. It informs the person of the contents liquid, gas, or solid state.
